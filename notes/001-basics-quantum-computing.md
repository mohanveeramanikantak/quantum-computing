# Quantum Computing – Detailed Notes

## 1. Introduction
Quantum computing is a revolutionary field that combines computer science with quantum mechanics. Unlike classical computers, which process data in binary (0s and 1s), quantum computers use qubits that can exist in multiple states simultaneously.

---

## 2. Classical vs Quantum Computing

### Classical Computing
- Uses bits (0 or 1)
- Sequential processing
- Limited by hardware scaling

### Quantum Computing
- Uses qubits
- Parallel computation via superposition
- Exponential speed potential

---

## 3. Key Principles

### 3.1 Superposition
A qubit can exist in a combination of states:
0 and 1 at the same time.

### 3.2 Entanglement
Two qubits can be linked, meaning the state of one instantly affects the other.

### 3.3 Quantum Interference
Used to amplify correct solutions and cancel wrong ones.

---

## 4. Qubits Explained
Qubits can be implemented using:
- Superconducting circuits  
- Trapped ions  
- Photons  

---

## 5. Quantum Gates

Common gates:
- Hadamard (H) → creates superposition  
- CNOT → creates entanglement  
- Pauli-X → flips state  

---

## 6. Quantum Algorithms

### 6.1 Shor’s Algorithm
Used for factoring large numbers (breaks encryption).

### 6.2 Grover’s Algorithm
Searches unsorted databases faster than classical methods.

---

## 7. Applications

### 7.1 Cryptography
Can break RSA encryption.

### 7.2 Healthcare
Drug discovery and molecular simulation.

### 7.3 Finance
Risk analysis and portfolio optimization.

### 7.4 Logistics
Route optimization and scheduling.

---

## 8. Challenges

- Quantum decoherence  
- Noise and error correction  
- Hardware complexity  
- High cost  

---

## 9. Future Scope (2045)

- Stable quantum computers  
- Integration with AI  
- Industry-wide adoption  
- New programming paradigms  

---

## 10. Career Scope

- Quantum Developer  
- Research Scientist  
- Physicist + Programmer hybrid  

---

## 11. Conclusion
Quantum computing has the potential to transform industries by solving problems that are currently impossible for classical systems. However, it is still in its early stages and requires significant advancements.
